Know Your Rights

Essential information about your constitutional rights during encounters with immigration enforcement. This is general information, not legal advice.

Your Rights if ICE Comes to Your Door

  • You do not have to open the door. ICE cannot enter without a judicial warrant signed by a judge (not an ICE administrative warrant).
  • Ask the agent to slide any warrant under the door. A valid judicial warrant will be signed by a judge and list your specific address.
  • An ICE administrative warrant (Form I-200 or I-205) does not give agents the right to enter your home.
  • You can say: "I do not consent to entry. Please slide any warrant under the door."

Your Rights if Stopped in Public

  • You have the right to remain silent. You do not have to answer questions about your immigration status, where you were born, or how you entered the country.
  • You can say: "I am exercising my right to remain silent."
  • Do not sign anything you do not understand.
  • Do not provide false documents. It is better to remain silent than to present false information.
  • You have the right to speak to a lawyer before signing anything or answering questions.

Your Rights if Detained

  • You have the right to a lawyer. Ask for one immediately. If you cannot afford a lawyer, ask for a list of free legal service providers.
  • You have the right to a hearing before an immigration judge.
  • You have the right to contact your consulate.
  • Do not sign any voluntary departure or other documents without consulting a lawyer.
  • Memorize or keep a lawyer's phone number accessible at all times.

At a Checkpoint or Traffic Stop

  • At border checkpoints (within 100 miles of any border), agents can ask about citizenship. You may choose to remain silent but this may prolong the stop.
  • Agents cannot search your vehicle without consent, a warrant, or probable cause.
  • You can say: "I do not consent to a search."
  • Stay calm and do not physically resist, even if you believe the stop is unlawful.

At Work (Workplace Raids)

  • ICE must have a judicial warrant to enter non-public areas of a workplace.
  • You have the right to remain silent and do not have to answer questions about your status.
  • You can ask: "Am I free to leave?" If yes, you may calmly walk away.
  • Document what happens: agent names, badge numbers, and agencies.

Prepare a Safety Plan

  • Keep important documents (IDs, birth certificates, immigration papers) in a safe, accessible location.
  • Memorize the phone number of an immigration lawyer or legal aid organization.
  • Designate a trusted person who can care for your children if needed.
  • Create a power of attorney for a trusted person to handle your affairs.
  • Teach your children their full name, your phone number, and the name of a trusted adult.
